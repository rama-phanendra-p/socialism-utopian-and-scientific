I. The Dreamers

Engels begins by analyzing the early "Utopian" socialists who first formulated a critique of the new capitalist world. They saw its injustices but, in an immature economic era, their solutions had to be "evolved out of the human brain."

Saint-Simon

A "son of the great French Revolution," he possessed a "masterly breadth of view."

  • Saw history as a class struggle between "workers" and "idlers."
  • Declared politics to be the "science of production."
  • Foretold the "absorption of politics by economics."
  • Proposed a society led by scientists and industrialists as "trustees of society."

Fourier

A "masterly" satirist who laid bare the "material and moral misery of the bourgeois world."

  • Wielded a witty, penetrating critique of civilization's "vicious circle."
  • Famously noted that "under civilization poverty is born of abundance itself."
  • First to declare woman's emancipation as the measure of general emancipation.

Robert Owen

A "born leader of men" who put his materialist philosophy into practice in a time of industrial chaos.

  • Created a model colony at New Lanark with humane working conditions.
  • Inventor of infant schools.
  • Advanced toward communism, identifying private property, religion, and marriage as key obstacles to reform.

II. The Method

To make socialism a science, Engels argued it required a new way of thinking: dialectics. This section contrasts this dynamic worldview with the static, "metaphysical" mode of thought it replaced.

The Method of Science

Proven by Darwin's evolution and modern physics.

Hegel revived dialectics but was an idealist. The "new facts" of class struggle led Marx to invert this, creating a **materialist dialectic**: explaining consciousness by material being, not being by consciousness. With this, socialism became a science.

III. The Engine

Engels argues that capitalism is driven by a "fundamental contradiction" which creates its wealth, its class divisions, and its inevitable crises. This is the engine of historical change.

The Fundamental Contradiction

Social Production

Production is a collective, social act performed by many workers together in a factory.

"Production itself changed from a series of individual operations into a series of social acts."
⬇️

Private Appropriation

But the product is owned individually by the capitalist, not by the workers who made it.

"The social product is appropriated by the individual capitalist. Fundamental contradiction..."
💥

The Result: Class Antagonism

This conflict between how goods are made (socially) and how they are owned (privately) manifests as the struggle between the Proletariat and the Bourgeoisie.

The Vicious Circle: A Crisis of Superabundance

This contradiction leads to periodic crises where "abundance becomes the source of distress and want." The system chokes on the very wealth it creates.
